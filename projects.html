Overview

A C programming coursework project to build an autonomous robot that navigates an 8×8 grid, locates markers scattered among obstacles, and returns each one to a defined home position. All 6 stages of the coursework were completed.

Key Highlights

  • Programmed a robot to navigate a dynamically generated 8×8 grid containing 5 obstacles and 3 markers, avoiding walls and collisions throughout.
  • Implemented a probability-based movement algorithm: 80% chance to move forward, 10% each for left/right turns; robot executes a left turn when facing an obstacle or wall.
  • Built an exact-path backtracking mechanism using a 2D array to store movements during marker searches, allowing the robot to retrace its steps precisely when returning home.
  • Structured the codebase across multiple modules: robot.c, animation.c, drawing.c, obstacle.c, marker.c, coordinates.c, and main.c.
  • Handled dynamic obstacle overlap scenarios where coincident random positions reduce the obstacle count, contributing to varied and complex map configurations.

Securing the Future: Post-Quantum & Quantum Cryptography

 Research Methods — UCL · Literature Review

PQCQKDCRYSTALS-KyberBB84NISTResearch

Overview

A literature review critically evaluating the two primary cryptographic paradigms emerging in response to the quantum computing threat: Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) and Quantum Key Distribution (QKD). The review examines how Shor's Algorithm renders foundational public-key standards such as RSA and ECC insecure, and assesses the urgency of migration driven by "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" (HNDL) attacks — where adversaries store today's encrypted traffic to decrypt once quantum capabilities mature. The paper concludes that while QKD offers superior long-term information-theoretic security for high-value critical infrastructure, PQC represents the only scalable and immediately deployable solution.

Key Highlights

  • Analysed the threat posed by Shor's Algorithm to widely deployed public-key systems (RSA, ECC), drawing on a 2025 SandboxAQ survey showing RSA still underpins ~11.5 million public keys on the internet.
  • Examined the HNDL (Harvest Now, Decrypt Later) threat model and applied the Mosca Inequality framework to evaluate the urgency of transitioning to quantum-secure schemes.
  • Studied CRYSTALS-Kyber (lattice-based PQC, NIST standardised): provides computational security compatible with existing classical infrastructure without requiring new hardware.
  • Studied BB84 (QKD): derives information-theoretic security guarantees from the fundamental laws of quantum mechanics, ensuring any interception attempt introduces detectable errors.
  • Conducted a comparative analysis across three dimensions: urgency of adoption, security foundations (computational hardness vs. information-theoretic), and practical deployment feasibility and infrastructure requirements.
